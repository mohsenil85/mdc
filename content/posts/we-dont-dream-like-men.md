One thing became apparent as I began to weave the body into my dreamwork: we don't dream like men.

We have monthly cycles that shift our hormones and shift our dreams. We have the power to gestate humans, which changes our sleep and the makeup of our dreams. We move through postpartum, perimenopause, and menopause — each one a portal that changes how we sleep and how we dream. All of these cycles shape the content and the texture of our nights, and learning to read those shifts is, I think, a quietly radical act.

## A little hormone literacy

Estrogen rises through the first half of your cycle and peaks around ovulation. After ovulation, the body produces progesterone — literally "pro-gestation," the hormone that readies and holds the womb lining for a possible pregnancy. If no pregnancy comes, both hormones fall, and that drop is what brings on the bleed.

Energetically, estrogen keeps things plump and outward-turned — more social, more engaged, more accommodating. When it falls, premenstrually and again in perimenopause, we tend to become less willing to people-please or to put up with the same levels of nonsense. Kate Codrington calls this the lifting of the "veil of estrogen."

## And a little sleep literacy

As our hormones shift, so does the architecture of our nights. In the luteal phase — the stretch before your period — many of us wake more often, and the more we surface from sleep, the more dreams we tend to carry back with us. After our period, in the follicular phase, we may sleep more soundly and spend more time in REM sleep, when dreams tend to arrive especially vivid.

Part of the practice is simply tracking this for yourself: noticing your quality of sleep and your dream recall across the different parts of your month. You may be surprised how much of a pattern there is.

## Dreaming with the cycle, not against it

Once you start to see the pattern, you can work *with* it. The late luteal and menstrual days, when our reserves are lower and our nervous systems sit closer to the edge, are not the time to plunge into your hardest material. The follicular and ovulatory days, when energy and resilience rise, can hold more.

This is the heart of the work: not one more thing to optimize, but a way of meeting yourself where you actually are — hormonally, somatically, honestly — on any given morning.
